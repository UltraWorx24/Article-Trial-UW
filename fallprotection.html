Introduction

Falls from height remain the leading cause of fatal workplace accidents in the UK, accounting for around a quarter of all work related deaths each year according to the HSE. They are an acute and immediate threat. For those responsible for rooftops, sites, or industrial facilities, fall protection is not a regulatory tick-box but a moral and professional duty.

In 2019, British Standards for fall protection were updated, raising the bar on compliance. Many older systems that once seemed “fine” may now fall short. Let’s break down what changed, the systems available, and how companies like Kee Safety help building managers keep up to date with the standard.

What Are Fall Protection Systems?

Fall protection systems are designed to either prevent a fall from occurring or minimise the consequences if one does happen. Common examples include fixed guardrails on rooftops, safety nets, personal fall arrest systems such as harnesses and lifelines, and permanent anchorage points built into the structure. Collective solutions like guardrails can be safely used by anyone ccessing a roof or raised platform, offering passive, hands free protection that does not depend on user action. In contrast, personal systems such as harnesses and lifelines provide discreet, low-profile safety across larger or more complex areas, but they require training and competence to use effectively.

Their purpose is simple: create safe access to edges, roofs, walkways, and fragile surfaces, while ensuring workers can carry out maintenance or inspections without unnecessary risk.

Why Compliance Changed in 2019

The revision of BS 7883:2019 was introduced to raise the standard of safety and accountability around personal fall protection equipment, particularly anchor systems. We as a company were regularly hearing of cases where so called inspections amounted to nothing more than a visual glance, with critical elements like eyebolts neither pull-tested nor dismantled to assess the integrity of the thread. It was becoming clear that change was urgently needed, too many untrained firms were carrying out “visual” inspections, signing systems off as safe when in reality workers lives were being put at risk. The updated standard responds to these concerns by making compliance more rigorous and transparent, ensuring anchor systems are properly designed, installed, maintained, and inspected by competent professionals, and giving dutyholders confidence that the equipment safeguarding their workforce truly meets a reliable benchmark for safety.

What Changed with Compliance: Key Updates in BS 7883:2019

BS 7883:2019 was brought in to reflect how much fall protection has moved on since the old 2005 standard. Back then, most systems revolved around simple eyebolts, but today a much wider range of anchors are used. The new standard recognises five main types from: permanent eyebolts and rigid rail systems to flexible lifelines, removable anchors, and even freestanding weights that rely on mass and friction. By setting out clear categories, it gives designers, installers, and inspectors a common language and a much clearer framework to work from.

A major focus of BS 7883:2019 is how inspection results are reported. Instead of vague sign offs, the standard sets out four clear outcomes:

  • Pass – The system meets all the requirements and is safe to use without restrictions.
  • Conditional Pass – The system is safe to use but does not fully meet every requirement. It must stay under review, with remedial work scheduled to bring it up to standard.
  • Conditional Fail – The system presents an immediate safety concern and must be taken out of service until the issue is fixed. Once repairs are made, a re-inspection is required before it can be used again.
  • Fail – The system is unsafe, cannot be used, and should be decommissioned. This outcome removes all doubt, making sure no one relies on equipment that poses a serious risk.

By introducing this structure, BS 7883:2019 takes away the grey areas that previously existed. Users now get a clear decision on every inspection, along with guidance on what needs to happen next.

Main Types of Fall Protection Systems

Type A – Permanently Fixed Anchors

Type A systems are the traditional form of fall protection and include permanently fixed anchors such as eyebolts. They are installed directly into the structure of a building or frame and provide a secure point of attachment for personal fall protection equipment.

Type B – Removable or Transportable Anchors

Type B systems are portable anchors that can be moved and used where needed, without being permanently fixed to a structure. Examples include tripod systems, deadweight anchors, or portable clamps.

Type C – Flexible Line Systems

Type C systems involve a flexible line, such as a wire rope, fibre rope, or webbing, that is tensioned and anchored at each end. These systems are often used for lifelines, providing users with the freedom to move horizontally across a wide area while remaining securely attached.

Type D – Rigid Rail Systems

Type D systems are rigid anchor systems, typically using a solid rail or tube fixed to the structure. They are often used in overhead fall arrest applications, such as in warehouses, transport depots, or aircraft hangars.

Type E – Freestanding Anchors

Type E systems rely purely on mass and friction to stay in place, rather than being fixed into the structure. Common examples include weighted trolley systems or deadweight anchors for flat roofs.

Practical Steps for Building & Safety Managers

If in doubt, always get your fall protection systems checked — never assume they are safe without proper evidence. The best place to start is with the original installer, who should be able to provide clear, straightforward reports that can be understood by anyone, not just technical specialists. If something in the paperwork or inspection outcome doesn’t make sense, it’s always better to ask than to overlook a potential risk. Just as importantly, make sure the company certifying your system is working to the latest industry standard, such as BS 7883:2019, so you can be confident your equipment is being assessed against the most up to date requirements for safety and compliance.
