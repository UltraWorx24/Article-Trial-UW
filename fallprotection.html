Introduction

In the world of building operations and safety management, preventing falls from height is non-negotiable. Falls remain one of the leading causes of workplace injuries and fatalities in the UK, with the HSE consistently reporting them as the number one cause of workplace deaths. For those responsible for sites, rooftops, or industrial facilities, ensuring compliance with fall protection systems isn’t just a legal box-tick—it’s about protecting lives and reputations.

In 2019, British Standards for fall protection were updated, raising the bar on compliance. Many older systems that once seemed “fine” may now fall short. Let’s break down what changed, the systems available, and how companies like Kee Safety help building managers keep pace.

What Are Fall Protection Systems?

Fall protection systems are engineered solutions that prevent workers from falling—or protect them if they do. They range from simple guardrails to advanced lifeline and anchorage systems. Unlike scaffolding or temporary measures, fall protection systems are usually permanent or semi-permanent fixtures integrated into the building or site design.

Their purpose is simple: create safe access to edges, roofs, walkways, and fragile surfaces, while ensuring workers can carry out maintenance or inspections without unnecessary risk.

Why Compliance Changed in 2019

The major update came with BS 7883:2019, the British Standard for the design, installation, and inspection of anchor systems for fall protection. This revision was introduced because too many installations were being certified without proper design calculations, documentation, or follow-up inspections. The 2019 update sought to close these gaps, making compliance more rigorous and transparent.

What Changed with Compliance: Key Updates in BS 7883:2019

Faster Setup and Teardown: Scaffolding often takes days—or even weeks—to assemble and dismantle. Rope access teams can be mobilized within hours, saving valuable time and reducing downtime for facilities.

Lower Equipment Costs: Rope access requires minimal gear compared to the heavy steel and labor-intensive nature of scaffolding. This translates to significant cost savings for clients.

Targeted Access: Rope access allows workers to reach only the specific area that needs attention, eliminating the need to scaffold an entire building or structure

Versatility in Facility Management

Rope access can be used across a wide range of tasks, including:

  • Exterior façade cleaning and repairs
  • Roof and gutter maintenance
  • HVAC and piping inspections
  • Painting and corrosion control
  • Structural surveys and safety inspections

Environmental Advantages

With a smaller physical footprint, rope access reduces the environmental impact of maintenance work. The method requires less material, transportation, and space—making it a more sustainable option for facility managers who are conscious of their environmental responsibilities

Conclusion

As facility management continues to evolve, rope access stands out as a safer, faster, and more economical solution compared to traditional scaffolding. Its adaptability across multiple tasks and commitment to safety makes it an indispensable tool in modern maintenance strategies. For organizations looking to optimize their operations while cutting costs and ensuring worker safety, rope access is not just an alternative—it’s the future of facility management.
