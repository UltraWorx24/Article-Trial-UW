Introduction

Working at height is intrinsically tied to the environment in which you operate. Too much rain makes the job significantly more difficult. Excessive wind can cause pendulum effects or dangerous movement from gusts. Freezing temperatures can halt all progress due to ice formation. Extreme heat brings human and equipment limitations into sharp focus. Learning your trade and understanding how environmental conditions affect your work can save money, time, injuries, and potentially lives.

Weather remains the largest uncontrollable factor in the working at height industry, which is why it must be studied and respected to maximise both productivity and safety. Our industry is incredibly safety-oriented, and rightly so. The consequences of incidents are usually severe and must be avoided at all costs. When work needs to be halted in favour of safety, executing this decision well and communicating the risks clearly to stakeholders becomes crucial for managing expectations.

Managing expectations is often the most challenging aspect of our work. Nothing is more frustrating than seeing teams unable to work, which makes clear communication critical to keeping everyone informed and satisfied with safety decisions. Minimising downtime is essential for successful relationships. Teams don't need to be on site when forecasts clearly indicate unsuitable conditions. Conversely, if forecasts predict workable conditions for time-critical jobs, having a presence before the operational window opens can maximize productivity during weather gaps.

Wind

Wind is the most common environmental factor preventing suitable working conditions across almost all height work jobs. Operational thresholds vary depending on working height and equipment type, and these limits must be specified by equipment manufacturers or health and safety trained personnel.

MEWPs and cranes typically begin de-rating around 9 m/s (18 knots), while facade work and rope access operations are often limited at 12 m/s (24 knots). It's crucial to remember that forecasts are usually given at 10 meters, but work is typically executed at greater heights.

Understanding wind shear is essential for interpreting how 10-meter forecasts affect your actual working height. This value changes depending on the air mass, making it important to access appropriate forecasting tools. Wind speeds can be up to 1.5 times higher at 30 meters depending on location and weather conditions. At 100 meters, you can easily see double the original forecasted value, completely invalidating operational thresholds based on standard weather app readings.

Wind force increases exponentially, so small increases in wind speed translate to significant increases in force on people and equipment. While the exact calculation depends on object shape, a 10% increase in wind speed can result in up to 20% more applied force. A 30% wind speed increase can cause nearly 70% more force on suspended personnel and objects.

Gusts are an inherent factor of wind speed, partially created by wind swirling around structures and changing speed and direction. This phenomenon mirrors wind tunnel turbulence but occurs on a much larger scale around the structures where we work. Most quality weather forecasts publish gust information for general areas, though they don't account for individual structures. Understanding site-specific gustiness patterns with varying speeds and directions can be key to operating in marginal conditions.

Anemometers are reasonably priced and should be standard equipment on every height work site. Whether permanently installed or handheld units, they represent good practice for validating forecasts with actual wind readings at the work area. These readings can inform protocol adjustments or prompt rescheduling work to less windy days.

Temperature Considerations

Temperature thresholds are equally important. Freezing temperatures create incredibly dangerous working environments through slippery surfaces and potential equipment functionality changes, especially when moisture is present.

Even when current temperatures aren't freezing, residual ice can persist, particularly on structures like bridges. Understanding site risks and ice formation conditions can prevent long journeys to frozen work sites on winter mornings.

Temperature thresholds work both ways. When conditions are too hot for safe work, there's little point in mobilizing to site. Heatwaves can cause heatstroke and heat exhaustion, which become incredibly serious in high-risk zones such as inaccessible areas or rope work environments.

Other Environmental Factors

Precipitation affects certain jobs by creating slippery conditions, impacting equipment operation, and making delicate work like electrical repairs significantly more difficult. Surfaces often remain wet long after rainfall stops, so conditions can be affected well beyond the actual precipitation event.

Lightning, often associated with summer storms, poses extreme danger to height workers. Since you're usually on or attached to one of the highest objects in the area, you become a common lightning target. Regardless of lightning rods or other mitigation measures, it's recommended to evacuate exposed situations within 20 kilometres of thunderstorms, as strikes have occurred up to 15 kilometres ahead of incoming storms.

When thunderstorm warnings are issued, a designated person should regularly monitor radar for lightning strikes and inform personnel accordingly. Once lightning strikes within 20 kilometres of the site, suspend activities until 30 minutes after the last strike leaves that zone. Establish clear lightning safety practices for your sites and equipment.

Human Factors

People's attitudes and performance change with weather conditions. Contractors are less likely to perform quality work when cold and wet compared to dry conditions where they're not eager to pack up immediately. While quality control measures and procedures exist, inexperienced operators sometimes take shortcuts to escape adverse conditions, leading to dangerous practices. Providing good waterproof and warm equipment alongside sensible working practices effectively mitigates these effects.

Professional Weather Assessment

There's a nearly endless list of weather phenomena that can affect rope work, making it difficult to identify each factor without occasionally overlooking fog chances or unexpected thunderstorms that completely derail plans. This is where professional meteorologists add value. They handle the research legwork and understand which conditions threaten specific sites or jobs. This expertise saves money during planning and resource allocation, increases safety through appropriate safeguards, and streamlines workflow by enabling informed decisions from reliable sources.

It's important to remember that even skilled meteorologists can't predict the future with absolute precision or specify exactly when rain will start or whether wind will exceed thresholds at specific times. This is where risk management becomes essential. While more scientific than gambling, uncertainty remains regarding exact timing and event intensity. A 40% rain chance means a 40% probability of that site experiencing precipitation but doesn't indicate intensity if it occurs. This information helps build probability pictures that site managers can use to assess risk and make decisions based on need and urgency.
